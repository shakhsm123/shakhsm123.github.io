Self-Play: From Game Theory Foundations to Self-Improving Language Models

A learning agent whose only teacher is a past or future version of itself. We trace the idea from Nash equilibria to modern PSV loops in LLMs.

~18.4k tokens · estimated 45 min read

Introduction

Most learning requires supervision: labeled examples, reward signals designed by humans, or demonstrations from experts. Self-play discards that requirement. The agent trains against itself — or copies of itself — generating its own curriculum, its own opposition, and its own feedback signal. The teacher and the student are the same entity at different points in training time.

The idea sounds circular, even paradoxical. How can a system improve by competing only against itself? The answer lies in a subtle asymmetry: the opponent is always the current best version of the agent. As the agent improves, so does the difficulty of the curriculum. This bootstrapping creates a never-ending ladder of increasing challenge, with no human in the loop.

Self-play became famous through DeepMind's AlphaGo and AlphaZero. But the mathematical foundations are much older, rooted in game theory and fixed-point theorems. And its most exciting frontier today is not board games — it is language models learning to reason, where the verification problem is far harder and the failure modes are more subtle.

This post is structured in four parts: foundations (theory and proofs), canonical architectures, a step-by-step implementation in two settings, and a survey of where the field is today. Code is in Python throughout; mathematical notation follows standard RL and game theory conventions.

Part I — Foundations

1.1 The game-theoretic view

Formally, consider a two-player zero-sum game defined by a tuple (S, A₁, A₂, R) where S is the state space, A₁ and A₂ are action spaces for players 1 and 2, and R : S × A₁ × A₂ → ℝ is the reward for player 1 (player 2 receives -R). A policy π : S → Δ(A) maps states to distributions over actions.

V*(s) = max_{π₁} min_{π₂} 𝔼[∑ᵢ γⁱ R(sᵢ, a¹ᵢ, a²ᵢ) | s₀=s, π₁, π₂]

The minimax theorem (von Neumann, 1928) guarantees that in finite two-player zero-sum games, the max and min operators commute — there exists a saddle point. Self-play is a computational procedure for finding this saddle point without explicit enumeration of the game tree.

In fictitious play (Brown, 1951), each player at time t best-responds to the empirical average of the opponent's historical strategies. Self-play in neural networks is a stochastic, parameterized version of this idea: instead of best responding to a historical mixture, we update the parameters of a single network against a copy of itself, periodically refreshing the opponent pool.

Key insight. In finite two-player zero-sum games, iterative self-play converges to a Nash equilibrium under mild assumptions on the update rule (e.g., no-regret learning). The rate of convergence depends on the structure of the game — tree games with perfect information converge faster than simultaneous games with hidden information.

The convergence argument works as follows. Define the exploitability of a policy π as the expected gain a best-responding opponent can achieve:

exploit(π) = max_{π'} V(π', π) − V(π, π)

A Nash equilibrium has exploit(π*) = 0. Under no-regret self-play, exploitability decreases at rate O(1/√T) where T is the number of iterations. This is the theoretical guarantee underlying AlphaZero's convergence.

1.2 The verification problem

Games have unambiguous outcomes. In chess, there is a winner. In Go, there is a score. The reward signal is cheap, binary, and perfectly reliable. This is the silent assumption baked into all of the above theory.

The verification problem is what happens when you remove this assumption. Suppose the agent is a language model and the task is mathematical reasoning. Verifying a final numerical answer is cheap — a symbolic checker suffices. But verifying a proof sketch, a plan, or a natural language argument is expensive, noisy, or impossible without a separate learned model.

Formally, let V : X × Y → {0, 1} be a verifier mapping (problem, solution) pairs to correctness judgments. Self-play is tractable when:

  1. V is exact: games, math answers, code execution.
  2. V is a learned model that itself improves during training.
  3. V is implicit in the structure of the environment (e.g., a game simulator).

The danger in case (2) is reward hacking: the agent finds solutions that fool the learned verifier without actually solving the problem. This is analogous to adversarial examples — the agent has found a blind spot in the verifier's distribution. A recent line of work (January 2026) formalizes when self-play is safe to use: the notion of learnable information gain requires that the self-generated training data contains structure not already captured in the model's current parameters.

Part II — Canonical Architectures

2.1 AlphaZero as the reference design

AlphaZero instantiates all three components cleanly. The policy-value network f_θ(s) = (p, v) outputs a prior over moves and a scalar value estimate. Monte Carlo Tree Search uses these to produce improved policy targets. The self-play loop generates games, which train the network, which improves MCTS, which generates better games.

Figure 1. The AlphaZero loop. Self-play generates (s, π_mcts, z) triples where z ∈ {-1, 0, 1} is the game outcome. The network is trained to minimize cross-entropy on π_mcts and MSE on z.

The key design choices: (a) the opponent is a recent snapshot of the network, not the current weights — this prevents destructive interference during training; (b) temperature annealing controls exploration vs exploitation in MCTS; (c) the value head provides a dense reward signal even in long games where the outcome is far in the future.

2.2 GANs as self-play

Generative adversarial networks (Goodfellow et al., 2014) are self-play between a generator G and a discriminator D. Recast in the two-player game frame: G is the proposer trying to fool D; D is the verifier trying to distinguish real from generated. The minimax objective is:

min_G max_D 𝔼_{x~p_data}[log D(x)] + 𝔼_{z~p_z}[log(1 − D(G(z)))]

The theoretical result (Goodfellow et al.) shows that the Nash equilibrium of this game is achieved when G recovers the data distribution exactly and D outputs 1/2 everywhere. In practice, training is notoriously unstable — mode collapse is the GAN analogue of self-play collapse in the LLM setting.

2.3 The Proposer–Solver–Verifier loop

Modern LLM self-play uses a triadic structure. A Proposer generates tasks; a Solver attempts them; a Verifier checks correctness. The Proposer and Solver are often the same model in different inference calls. The loop: 

for iteration t = 1 ... T:
    tasks = Proposer.generate(difficulty_level=t)
    solutions = Solver.attempt(tasks)
    rewards = Verifier.check(tasks, solutions)
    update Solver with (tasks, solutions, rewards)
    update Proposer curriculum based on Solver's pass rate

The curriculum is the hard part. If the Proposer generates tasks the Solver always fails, the gradient signal is zero. If the Proposer generates tasks the Solver always passes, the signal is also zero. The sweet spot — tasks the Solver passes roughly 50–80% of the time — produces the strongest learning signal.

Collapse. Systems like R-Zero report rapid initial gains followed by instability after a few iterations. The Proposer drifts toward trivially easy or unsolvable tasks because both extremes are locally optimal for minimizing the training loss of the Proposer itself. Stabilization requires either a fixed external task distribution or a learned difficulty signal that is robust to hacking.

    def run_episode(self):
        game = Connect4()
        history = []
        while True:
            s = game.board.copy()
            # current player uses live net; opponent uses frozen snapshot
            if game.current_player == 1:
                p, v = self.net(encode(s))
            else:
                p, v = self.opponent(encode(s))
            a = sample(p, game.legal_moves())
            result = game.drop(a)
            history.append((s, p.detach(), game.current_player))
            if result is not None:
                self._assign_rewards(history, result)
                break

    def update_opponent(self):
        # refresh frozen opponent every N iterations
        # this prevents destructive interference
        self.opponent = copy.deepcopy(self.net)

3.2 Setting 2: LLM math reasoning (PSV toy loop)

We now port the same skeleton to a language model setting. The Proposer generates arithmetic problems of varying difficulty; the Solver (same model, different prompt) attempts them; the Verifier is a Python eval() call — perfectly reliable, zero cost. 

import anthropic

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def propose(difficulty: int) -> str:
    resp = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-20250514",
        max_tokens=100,
        messages=[{
            "role": "user",
            "content": f"Generate a math problem with difficulty {difficulty}/10. "
                       "Return only the problem statement, no solution."
        }]
    )
    return resp.content[0].text

def solve(problem: str) -> str:
    resp = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-20250514",
        max_tokens=200,
        messages=[{
            "role": "user",
            "content": f"Solve this problem. Return only the final numeric answer: {problem}"
        }]
    )
    return resp.content[0].text.strip()

def verify(problem: str, answer: str) -> bool:
    # extract numeric answer and check with symbolic solver
    try:
        import sympy
        return abs(float(sympy.sympify(answer)) - ground_truth(problem)) < 1e-6
    except:
        return False

# PSV loop
for iteration in range(20):
    difficulty = calibrate_difficulty(recent_pass_rate)
    problems = [propose(difficulty) for _ in range(batch_size)]
    answers  = [solve(p) for p in problems]
    rewards  = [verify(p, a) for p, a in zip(problems, answers)]
    pass_rate = sum(rewards) / len(rewards)
    log(iteration, difficulty, pass_rate)

The key diagnostic is the pass rate as a function of proposed difficulty. A well-calibrated Proposer keeps pass rate in the 0.5–0.8 range. Collapse shows up as the pass rate jumping to 0.95+ (trivial tasks) or dropping to 0.05 (impossible tasks) within a few iterations.

Part IV — Current Research Landscape

The most active area of self-play research today is not games — it is reasoning in language models. Several threads are worth following:

Math and code as the beachhead. Verifiable domains let researchers deploy exact verifiers, making self-play tractable without a learned reward model. DeepSeek-R1, R-Zero, and related systems all exploit this: train on problems where correctness is checkable, use self-play to generate harder problems, iterate.

Learnable information gain. A February 2026 paper formalizes when self-play is theoretically sound. The condition is that the generated data must have nonzero learnable information gain relative to the current model — it must contain structure the model cannot already predict. This gives a diagnostic for detecting collapse before it happens.

Adversarial imitation learning equivalence. The same paper shows that adversarial self-play (one model generates, one model discriminates) is mathematically equivalent to adversarial imitation learning. This connects the self-play literature to a separate line of imitation learning research.

Autonomous driving. Self-play in simulation (Gigaflow, 2025) enables autonomous driving agents to learn without any human demonstrations. The agents compete against each other in diverse scenarios, discovering corner cases that human-designed curricula miss.

The central open question remains: under what conditions does iterative self-play lead to genuine intelligence improvement, rather than increasingly sophisticated reward hacking? The field is beginning to answer this mathematically rather than empirically — which is the sign of a maturing area.
